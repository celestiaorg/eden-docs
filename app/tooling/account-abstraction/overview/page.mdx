Account abstraction (AA) makes your app easier to use by providing a familiar user experience (UX) and simplifying complex interactions for users.

## tl;dr

Account abstraction (AA) makes onchain apps feel like web2: simple login, one-click actions, token fees, and safer flows.

- **Onboarding:** passkey/email login, easy recovery
- **Actions:** bundle txs into one confirmation
- **Fees:** gas covered or paid in tokens
- **Safety:** limits, allowlists, session keys
- **Recovery:** built-in device/account recovery

**Patterns:** sponsored txs (no pre-funding, clear costs), operations (intent-first, one confirm), session keys (scoped, short-lived signers).

## How does AA improve UX?

- **Smooth onboarding:** Sign in without a seed phrase using passkeys, email, or phone number instead. These wallets are instantly recoverable.
- **Operations vs. transactions:** Put multiple calls into one "operation" with a clear purpose and one confirmation.
- **Simplified fees:** Paymasters can either pay for gas or let users pay with other tokens. This helps show the exact costs up front.
- **Safety rails:** Make rules for spending limits, allowlists, and session keys to cut down on repetitive prompts.

## 1. Sponsored transactions (gas sponsorship)

“Gasless” doesn’t mean _free_, it means your user doesn't have to figure out how to have TIA before they even understand they should have a wallet. With smart accounts ([ERC-4337](https://eips.ethereum.org/EIPS/eip-4337)), a **paymaster** can sponsor a user’s gas so the flow feels like a web2 checkout with “free shipping.” This is how you turn “I don’t have tokens” into “one click sign up.”

### Why sponsor gas? (and how to do it well)

- **No prerequisite funding:** remove the “buy native tokens first” blocker so users can start right away.
- **Predictable costs:** communicate clear, stable pricing up front (“free”, fixed token amounts, or “covered by Eden”) instead of volatile base fees.
- **Fewer prompts:** batch multi-step flows (mints, swaps, quests) into a single confirmation.
- **Graceful fallback:** if sponsorship isn’t available, offer a clear alternative (“pay 0.01 TIA” or “try again later”).

### How ERC-4337 sponsorship works

1. User signs a **UserOperation** (no native tokens required).
2. The application includes **paymaster data** proving that someone will cover the gas.
3. Bundler submits the operation to EntryPoint.
4. During `validatePaymasterUserOp` and `postOp`, the paymaster proves eligibility and settles the gas cost (either from its deposit or by collecting a token reimbursement).

Think of a paymaster as the smart account’s “company card.” You set the rules; the bundler deducts from your balance when ops go through.

### Paymaster options you’ll see today

- **Verifying paymaster (a.k.a. sponsored gas)**
  Your backend or policy decides _which_ calls you’ll pay for (e.g., free mint, first swap, weekly cap). Users sign a UserOperation; the paymaster validates it and the bundler pulls fees from the paymaster’s deposit.

- **Token paymaster (“stablecoin gas”)**
  Users pay fees in a token they already hold (USDC, in-game token). The paymaster fronts the native gas, then collects reimbursement in that token during `postOp`. UX: show the token fee, not the network coin.

<Callout>
  **Both models are available through ZeroDev today**: you configure policies in the dashboard, and
  the SDK handles the UserOperation wiring for you.
</Callout>

### Designing your sponsorship policy

- **Eligibility:** which contracts/functions qualify? Are there per-user or per-app quotas? ZeroDev policies let you filter by target address, calldata, and more.
- **Risk controls:** rate limits, allowlists/deny lists, daily budget caps. Combine onchain policy checks with backend analytics.
- **Funding plan:** keep the verifying paymaster deposit topped up; simulate expected burn before launch.
- **Observability:** track pending/included statuses of operations, alert on stuck ops, and log every sponsored action for audits.

#### Quick checklist (prod-minded)

- [ ] Define **eligibility**: which contracts/functions are sponsored, and when.
- [ ] Add **caps & rate limits**: per user, per day, per action.
- [ ] Provide a **fallback**: if sponsorship is exhausted, switch to token gas or prompt for native.
- [ ] **Observe** the lifecycle: surface “submitted → included” statuses; alert on stuck ops.
- [ ] **Fund & monitor** the paymaster deposit; set alerts for low balance.
- [ ] Document the user-facing messaging (“Gas covered by Eden” vs. “You’ll pay 0.25 USDC”).

<Callout type="warning">
  Avoid “sponsor all transactions” in production. Scope sponsorship to specific actions and add
  per-user limits to prevent abuse.
</Callout>

## 2. Fewer transactions, more operations

Instead of designing for raw transactions, design for **operations** (what the user intends). In order for users to confirm **once** and proceed, an operation can conceal fees through sponsorship or token gas, bundle several steps, and use a session key to prevent repeated prompts and save the user's time. Operations are the heart of the UX improvements account abstraction brings.

<Callout type="info">
  Under the hood, operations are powered by account abstraction (ERC-4337). Your app creates a
  signed request, a bundler submits it to the EntryPoint contract, and the smart account executes
  your batched calls. We’ll keep those details out of the UI, though.
</Callout>

### What makes an operation?

**One confirmation, many steps:** such as _approve + swap_, _mint + list_, and _wrap + stake_.

- **Optional sponsorship:** pay token-denominated fees or network fees.
  To prevent users from signing each click, use a **session key**.
  **First-touch deploy:** If the smart account hasn't been deployed yet, deploy it and follow the same procedure.
  **Deterministic preview:** displays the smart account address _prior to_ its onchain existence.

### UX flow (recommended)

<Steps>
### Compose the action

- Collect inputs (amounts, recipient, slippage).
- If the account is counterfactual, **preview the address**.

#### Human-readable preview

- Show _what will happen_ (tokens in/out, contract names, recipients).
- Flag risks (large approvals, high slippage, unknown recipients).

#### Confirm once

- Optional: let users **“remember this session”** (create a short-lived session key).

#### Submit & track

- Show states: **Submitted → Processing → Included**.
- Provide a single status component with retry/cancel when appropriate.

#### Receipt & next step

- Link to explorer when included.
- Suggest the next action (e.g., “List now”, “Share”, “Deposit more”).

</Steps>

### Reusable patterns

- **Approve + operation in one go**
  Replace two prompts with one batched call; cap approval to the needed amount.
- **Mint + list**
  Create, then immediately list on a marketplace in one operation.
- **Wrap + stake**
  Wrap native → stake contract; show a single preview of the final position.
- **First-touch deploy**
  Deploy the smart account and perform the action together; don’t make “create wallet” a separate step.

### Status & errors (what to surface)

- **Submitted**: we’ve sent your operation to the network.
- **Processing**: simulation and bundling in progress (can take a few seconds).
- **Included**: onchain; show explorer link.
- **Couldn’t process**: explain plainly:
  - Budget not sponsored (try again with token gas or native fee).
  - Invalid inputs / simulation failed (adjust amounts/slippage).
  - Rate/budget limits reached (try later or reduce size).

## 3. Session keys

**Goal:** Design flows that feel instant, no repeat sign pop-ups, while keeping users inside safe guardrails.

- **Session keys** are additional validators that a smart account creates with scoped policies. They aren’t a special account type or a consensus node, think of them as temporary agents that can sign only within the limits you configure (what, how often, how much, and for how long).

### A look at the architecture

- **Owner vs. agent:** Kernel accounts (EntryPoint 0.7 / Kernel v3+) expose a “sudo” validator (owner) and any number of “regular” validators (agents). A session key is an agent that can act only inside the constraints approved by the owner.
- **Installation flow:**
  1. Agent creates a key pair (or remote signer) and shares the **session key address** with the owner.
  2. Owner composes policies (timestamp, call target, spend/rate caps, etc.), installs the permission plugin, and signs the approval.
  3. Agent receives the serialized approval (`serializePermissionAccount`) and combines it with the key to execute operations via `deserializePermissionAccount`.
- **Revocation:** The owner (sudo validator) _uninstalls_ the plugin. Keep the serialized approval retrievable; you need it to uninstall the exact validator instance.

### When to use session keys

- **Use session keys** for: gameplay sessions, one-click claims, checkout flows, background actions while a tab is open, mobile device sessions.

### UX wins

- **Fewer prompts:** Gameplay loops, claims, or checkout steps advance programatically after the initial approval.
- **Predictable progress:** Long-running or automated actions can finish in the background while the user keeps interacting, like a game or social media app.
- **Confidence & control:** Scoped permissions (contract + function + value caps) lower the fear of "signing everything," and users can pause or revoke without having to touch their main key

### Designing the grant (owner approval)

- **Scope:** target contracts and function selectors; optional calldata guards via policies such as `toCallPolicy` with ABI filters.
- **Limits:** set limits on the value of each call and the total value of all calls, and use `toRateLimitPolicy` (e.g., 3 calls/hour) to stop runaway loops.
- **Lifetime:** timestamp policies limit the start and end window; combine this with a frontend UX that makes expiry stand out.
- **Replay protection:** the permissions plugin keeps an isolated nonce space, so each session key uses its own counter to avoid replay.
- **Surface area:** show a preview that people can read, like "Can call `mint()` on MagicBadge(0xA1B2C3...beef) up to 3 times today; gas paid for by Eden."

### The lifecycle and UI

Grant → Use → Monitor → Revoke/Expire → Audit

Show the last activity, the active sessions, and the budget left. Give users the option to **Revoke** (sudo uninstall) and **Extend** (reissue approval) with just one click.<br/> For automations that run on servers, show webhooks or notifications when usage gets close to its limits.

### Quick checklist

- [ ] Set short TTLs (less than 24 hours) and small scopes by default.
- [ ] Use policies and backend monitoring to enforce "caps and rate limits" for each session.
- [ ] Keep each session's "nonces" separate so they can't be replayed.
- [ ] Keep the **serialized approval** safe, just like you would a capability token. Do not leave it (or the session private key) unencrypted in localStorage.
- [ ] Think about using "remote signers" or a trusted backend to store session keys. Before giving someone signing rights, they should have to enter a passkey or two-factor authentication.
- [ ] Keep a record of **who/what/when** for every use and make an audit view available.
- [ ] Support "off-chain checks": allow EIP-1271 signatures so that backend/API calls can check through the smart account.

<Callout type="warning">
  Avoid blanket “*can call anything*” grants. If you must, limit by time and budget, and require an
  additional confirmation for dangerous functions (e.g., upgrades, ownership transfers).
</Callout>

<Callout type="info">
  Put approvals and session keys in different areas of trust. If a frontend needs to store data, it
  should encrypt it in memory and delete it when the user logs out or the time runs out.
</Callout>
